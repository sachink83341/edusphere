Tenses

Understanding Past, Present, and Future

Present Tense

Simple Present: I write letters every day.

Present Continuous: I am writing a letter now.

Present Perfect: I have written three letters today.

Present Perfect Continuous: I have been writing for two hours.

Past Tense

Simple Past: I wrote a letter yesterday.

Past Continuous: I was writing when you called.

Past Perfect: I had written the letter before you arrived.

Past Perfect Continuous: I had been writing for hours.

Future Tense

Simple Future: I will write a letter tomorrow.

Future Continuous: I will be writing at 3 PM.

Future Perfect: I will have written the letter by evening.

Future Perfect Continuous: I will have been writing for three hours by then.

Tense Usage Tips

  • • Present Perfect for completed actions affecting present
  • • Past Perfect for actions before another past action
  • • Future Perfect for actions completed before a future time
  • • Continuous forms show ongoing actions

Modal Verbs

Express possibility, ability, permission, and obligation

Can / Could

Ability:

• I can swim. (present ability)

• I could swim when I was young. (past ability)

Permission:

• Can I use your phone? (informal)

• Could I borrow your book? (polite)

May / Might

Permission:

• May I come in? (formal)

Possibility:

• It may rain today. (50% chance)

• It might rain today. (less certain)

Must / Have to

Obligation:

• You must wear a seatbelt. (strong obligation)

• I have to go to work. (external obligation)

Certainty:

• He must be tired. (logical conclusion)

Should / Ought to

Advice:

• You should exercise regularly.

• You ought to be more careful.

Expectation:

• The train should arrive at 5 PM.

Passive Voice

Converting active sentences to passive form

Formation: Subject + be + Past Participle (+ by agent)

Active Voice
  • • John writes letters.
  • • Mary is cooking dinner.
  • • They have built a house.
  • • She will complete the project.
Passive Voice
  • • Letters are written by John.
  • • Dinner is being cooked by Mary.
  • • A house has been built by them.
  • • The project will be completed by her.

When to Use Passive Voice

  • • When the doer is unknown: "The window was broken."
  • • When the action is more important: "The vaccine was developed."
  • • In formal writing: "Mistakes were made."
  • • To avoid responsibility: "The deadline was missed."

Passive with Different Tenses

  • • Present: The letter is written.
  • • Past: The letter was written.
  • • Future: The letter will be written.
  • • Present Perfect: The letter has been written.

Direct & Indirect Speech

Reporting what someone said

Direct Speech

Exact words spoken, enclosed in quotation marks.

• He said, "I am happy."

• She asked, "Where are you going?"

• They said, "We will come tomorrow."

Indirect Speech

Reported speech without quotation marks.

• He said that he was happy.

• She asked where I was going.

• They said that they would come the next day.

Changes in Indirect Speech

Pronouns
  • I → he/she
  • you → I/he/she
  • we → they
  • my → his/her
Tenses
  • present → past
  • past → past perfect
  • will → would
  • can → could
Time/Place
  • today → that day
  • tomorrow → the next day
  • yesterday → the day before
  • here → there

Articles

Using A, An, and The correctly

Indefinite Article "A"

Used before words beginning with consonant sounds:

  • • A book
  • • A university (sounds like "yoo")
  • • A one-way street
  • • A European country

Indefinite Article "An"

Used before words beginning with vowel sounds:

  • • An apple
  • • An honest man (silent 'h')
  • • An umbrella
  • • An hour (silent 'h')

Definite Article "The"

Used for specific nouns:

  • • The book I gave you
  • • The sun, the moon
  • • The first time
  • • The United States

No Article Needed

General plural nouns, abstract nouns, proper nouns, meals, languages, sports, subjects

Examples: Books are useful. Life is beautiful. I study English. I play football.

Determiners

Words that introduce and specify nouns

Demonstrative

Point to specific nouns:

  • • This book (singular, near)
  • • That car (singular, far)
  • • These books (plural, near)
  • • Those cars (plural, far)

Possessive

Show ownership:

  • • My house
  • • Your friend
  • • His/Her bag
  • • Our/Their school

Quantifiers

Express quantity:

  • • Some/Any milk
  • • Many/Few books
  • • Much/Little water
  • • All/Most students

Conjunctions

Connecting words, phrases, and clauses

Coordinating Conjunctions

Connect equal parts (FANBOYS):

  • • For: I stayed home, for it was raining.
  • • And: I like tea and coffee.
  • • Nor: He doesn't smoke, nor does he drink.
  • • But: I'm tired, but I'll continue.
  • • Or: Would you like tea or coffee?
  • • Yet: It's expensive, yet worth it.
  • • So: I was late, so I missed the bus.

Subordinating Conjunctions

Connect dependent to independent clauses:

  • • Because: I left because it was late.
  • • Although: Although tired, I continued.
  • • When: Call me when you arrive.
  • • If: If it rains, we'll stay inside.
  • • Since: Since you're here, let's start.
  • • Unless: Unless you hurry, you'll be late.
  • • While: While studying, I listen to music.
